# Gradient Descent

The deepest intuition for gradient descent is this: imagine you’ve been blindfolded and dropped onto a hilly landscape. Your only goal is to reach the lowest valley. You can’t see anything, but you can feel the slope under your feet right where you’re standing. So you do the only logical thing — feel which way is downhill, take a small step in that direction, stop, feel again, repeat. That’s gradient descent, exactly and completely.

The “landscape” is your loss function L(w) — a surface measuring how wrong your model is. “Downhill” means reducing that loss. The slope you feel is the gradient dL/dw, the derivative of the loss. And “taking a step downhill” is the update rule. One formula rules everything:

w ← w − α · (dL/dw)

where α (alpha) is the learning rate — how big each step is. The minus sign is crucial: the gradient points uphill, so you subtract it to go down. Let’s make this completely concrete and visceral:—

# The gradient: what it actually is

The gradient dL/dw is just the derivative of your loss function — the slope of the curve at exactly the point where you stand. If it’s positive (curve going up to the right), you need to move left (decrease w). If it’s negative (curve going down to the right), you need to move right (increase w). The update rule w ← w − α · dL/dw handles both cases automatically: subtracting a positive gradient moves w left, subtracting a negative gradient moves w right. You always end up stepping toward the valley floor.

In higher dimensions (real neural networks), the gradient becomes a vector — one derivative per parameter. A neural network with 1 million parameters has a gradient that is a vector of 1 million numbers, one for each weight, all computed simultaneously via backpropagation. But the update rule is identical: subtract the gradient vector scaled by the learning rate.

# The learning rate: the single knob that can ruin everything

Try this in the widget: drag the learning rate slider all the way left (tiny α), press Play, and watch. The ball inches toward the minimum painfully slowly — it’ll take thousands of steps. Now drag it all the way right and watch the ball shoot past the minimum, overshoot wildly to the other side, then overshoot back even worse. This is the three-regime understanding you must internalize:

When α is too small, the steps are so tiny that you need an impractical number of iterations to converge, and you may waste enormous computation. When α is just right, you take confident steps that close in on the minimum efficiently. When α is too large, each step overshoots the minimum so severely that the ball lands on the opposite wall higher than it started — and the next step overshoots even further. This runaway process is divergence, and it’s one of the most common training failures in real ML. The loss goes to infinity rather than zero.

The “bumpy valley” function in the widget illustrates a second cruel problem: local minima. Try starting from the right side of the parameter range — the ball rolls into the shallow local minimum on the right and stops there, completely unaware of the deeper global minimum on the left. Gradient descent is blind to the global structure; it only sees the slope under its feet. This is why modern neural network training uses techniques like momentum, learning rate schedules, and random restarts to escape such traps.

# From 1 dimension to 1 billion parameters

In the widget, w is a single number. In real neural networks, w is a vector with potentially billions of components. Nothing about the algorithm changes — you just compute all the partial derivatives at once (that’s what backpropagation does efficiently using the chain rule) and apply the same update to every parameter simultaneously:

w⃗ ← w⃗ − α · ∇L(w⃗)

where ∇L is now a gradient vector, not a scalar. The loss surface has billions of dimensions instead of one, which is impossible to visualize, but the principle is identical to the ball on the curve.

# The complete mental model, cemented

You now have everything. The loss function is the landscape. The parameters are your coordinates on that landscape. The gradient is the slope under your feet. The learning rate is your stride length. The update rule w ← w − α · ∇L(w) is the act of stepping downhill. Convergence is reaching the valley floor where the gradient vanishes. The only things that can go wrong are: stepping too small (slow convergence), stepping too large (divergence), or the landscape having local traps (you converge, but to the wrong valley).

Every optimizer you’ll ever meet — Adam, RMSProp, AdaGrad, SGD with momentum — is gradient descent with some clever modification to the step size or direction. They all share this one core update. Once you see that, the entire optimization literature becomes much less intimidating.

# ⚠️ Tricky Interview Facts

  1. Local minima are rarely the real problem in deep networks — saddle points and plateaus are. Most local minima in high-dim spaces have loss values close to the global minimum.
  2. Larger batch size ≠ always better — empirically, small batches act as implicit regularizers (sharp vs. flat minima); flat minima generalize better (Keskar et al., 2017).
  3. Adam doesn’t always generalize as well as SGD — Adam converges faster but SGD with tuned lr/momentum often reaches lower test loss; common to switch to SGD late in training.
