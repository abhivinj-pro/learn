Precision Recall - CTR

The core framing: Your model decides: “Should I show this card to this user?” The “positive” prediction means: “This user will click.”

Here’s how the 4 outcomes map to your world : Here’s the key insight your situation reveals:

Precision = your CTR, literally. The formula clicks / total seen is exactly TP / (TP + FP). Optimizing CTR is optimizing precision. Your model’s job is to suppress FPs — people who see the card but don’t click.

Recall = your revenue protection. You said “I don’t want to lose many clicks.” That’s recall — of all the users who would have clicked, how many did your model actually show the card to? A model that only shows the card to 10 people with a 100% CTR is useless if 500 real clickers never saw it.

The trap you identified is real. You noticed that simply hiding the card from more people raises CTR. That’s trivially true — but it’s precision without recall. Your model could achieve 100% CTR by showing the card to only one guaranteed clicker. That’s not a model, that’s a filter.

What you actually need is a model operating at the right point on the precision-recall curve — where CTR is above whatever threshold keeps the card alive, while recall stays high enough that you’re not bleeding potential clicks. In practice, this means:

  • Set a minimum precision target (e.g. CTR ≥ 8%) — this is your spam protection floor.
  • Then maximize recall subject to that constraint — this is your business objective.
  • The slider metaphor in the widget shows exactly this: drag it toward strict until CTR clears the floor, and no further.

This framing — “maximize recall, subject to a precision floor” — is the right way to think about your model’s success metric, not accuracy, not F1 by default.
