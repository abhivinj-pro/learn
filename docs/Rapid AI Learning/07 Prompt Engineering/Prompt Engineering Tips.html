Prompt Engineering Tips

# Task: Be Specific About What You Want

A task isn’t a topic it’s the exact output you need. Most people stay too vague.

Wrong: “Help me with marketing.”

Right: “Write three email subject lines for a product launch targeting small business owners.”

The first gives the AI nowhere to go. The second gives it a precise target.

# Persona: Activate the Right Expertise

When you assign a persona, you’re not role-playing. You’re steering the AI toward specific vocabulary, reasoning, and emphasis.

Wrong: “Act as an expert and help me with my startup.”

Right: “Act as a venture capitalist who has evaluated 500 seed-stage pitches. Identify the three biggest weaknesses a skeptical VC would notice in my positioning.”

Generic personas do nothing. Specific ones shape the entire response.

# Format: Stop Reformatting AI Output

If you don’t specify format, you get walls of text you have to reorganize. Specify exactly what container you want.

Wrong: “Give me ideas for improving customer retention.”

Right: “Provide five customer retention strategies in a numbered list. For each: (1) tactic name, (2) one-sentence description, (3) implementation difficulty 1-5, (4) expected timeline.”

The second prompt produces output you can use immediately.

# Context: The More You Give, The Less AI Guesses

Context is background information that steers toward relevance. Without it, the AI assumes and assumptions mean generic output.

Wrong: “Write a job description for a software engineer.”

Right: “Write a job description for a mid-level backend engineer. We’re a 30-person healthcare startup. Requires Python and HIPAA familiarity. Small team, so independence matters. Tone: warm and direct corporate language attracts wrong applicants.”

# References: Show, Don’t Tell

When words can’t capture what you want, show examples instead.

Wrong: “Write a LinkedIn post in a professional but engaging tone.”

Right: “Here are three LinkedIn posts I wrote that performed well: [paste posts]. Analyze what makes them effective, then write a new post about remote work productivity following the same patterns.”

The AI matches patterns it can observe better than patterns you describe.

# Evaluate: Actually Check the Output

Most people skim results and settle for “good enough.” That’s a mistake.

After every important response, ask: Does this hit my task? Is the tone right? Are the facts accurate? Would I use this as-is?

If any answer is no, you know what to fix next.

# Iterate: Prompting Is a Loop

Your first prompt is rarely your last. Four ways to fix broken prompts:

Revisit the framework. Check if you missed context or forgot a persona.

Break into simpler sentences. Dense paragraphs overwhelm. One instruction per sentence.

Try analogous tasks. If “write a business proposal” gives dry results, try “write a persuasive argument for why Company X should partner with us.”

Add constraints. Limits force specificity. “Must be under 90 seconds, must focus on one tip, must start with a question.”

# Multimodal Prompting: Beyond Text

Modern models process images, audio, and video directly. Same principles apply you’re just replacing text descriptions with direct input.

Want website feedback? Upload the screenshot instead of describing the layout. The AI analyzes what it sees, not your description of it.

# Hallucinations and Bias: Know the Limits

AI models confidently generate false information and reflect biases from training data. Neither problem is solved.

The concept: human in the loop. You’re responsible for final output. Treat AI results as first drafts requiring verification, not final authority.

# Prompt Chaining: Build Complexity in Steps

Use the output of one prompt as input for the next. Complex projects work better in stages than all-at-once.

Example chain for a marketing strategy:

  1. Analyze competitive landscape
  2. Based on that, identify three positioning opportunities
  3. For the best one, develop messaging framework
  4. Using that framework, draft landing page copy

Each step builds on the previous. Quality stays high because each prompt focuses on one thing.

# Chain of Thought: Make AI Show Its Work

Ask the AI to reason step-by-step instead of jumping to conclusions. You catch flawed logic and often get better answers.

Standard: “Which pricing model should I use for my app?”

Chain of thought: “I’m choosing between freemium, subscription, and one-time purchase for my app. Walk through your analysis step by step, considering user acquisition, revenue, and competitive dynamics. Then recommend a model and explain why.”

# Tree of Thought: Explore Multiple Paths

For problems without one right answer, have the AI develop several approaches simultaneously.

Example: “Generate three different onboarding approaches for my app: one prioritizing speed, one prioritizing education, one prioritizing personalization. For each, explain the user experience and likely drop-off points.”

You see the landscape of possibilities, not just one recommendation.

# AI Agents: Reusable Systems for Recurring Tasks

An agent is a defined system for specific tasks, not a one-time prompt.

Simulation agent (practice partner): “You’re a skeptical purchasing manager. I’ll pitch my software. Respond realistically raise objections, ask hard questions. After we finish, give feedback on what worked.”

Expert feedback agent: “You’re an editor with 15 years at business publications. Critique my drafts for: clarity, jargon, structure, headlines. Be direct and specific.”

Build agents by combining: specific persona + your context + interaction pattern + ending condition.

# Meta prompting: Use AI to Fix Your Prompts

When stuck, ask the AI to improve your prompt.

“I’m trying to generate social media content but getting generic results. Here’s my prompt: [paste]. What’s missing? Rewrite it to be more effective.”

The AI identifies gaps you might not notice.

# The Bottom Line

Good prompting isn’t magic words. It’s structured communication: clear task, useful persona, enough context, specified format, relevant references. Then evaluate and iterate.

The people who complain AI doesn’t work are the ones typing vague sentences and hoping for the best.
