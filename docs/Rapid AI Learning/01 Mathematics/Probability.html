# 2. Bayes’ Theorem

# The Core Idea in One Sentence

Bayes’ theorem is about updating your belief when you get new evidence.

That’s it. Everything else is just math wrapped around that idea.

# Start Here: You Already Do This Every Day

You wake up and look outside. The sky is dark and cloudy.

You think: “Hmm, it might rain today.”

Then you check your phone: “Your outdoor concert tonight is still on.”

You think: “They wouldn’t keep it on if rain was likely. Maybe it won’t rain.”

You just did Bayes. You started with a belief (might rain), got new evidence (concert is on), and updated your belief (probably won’t rain).

Bayes’ theorem is just the precise mathematical recipe for doing what your brain already does naturally.

# The Detective Analogy

You’re Sherlock Holmes.

A man walks in. Before he speaks, what’s the probability he’s a murderer? Very low — maybe 1 in 10,000. That’s your prior belief.

Now he speaks. Mud on his boots. A scratch on his hand. Nervous eyes.

Each clue updates your belief upward. By the end, maybe 1 in 3.

That journey — from 1/10,000 → 1/3, driven by evidence — is Bayes’ theorem.

But here’s the question most people never ask: why doesn’t every clue update you by the same amount?

Because of how common the clue is. Mud on boots means a lot in a luxury hotel lobby. It means almost nothing on a construction site. The same evidence has different power depending on how often it shows up in the general world.

# The Four Ingredients

Bayes has four, and the fourth is the one everyone skips.

  lik -->|"÷"| evid["EVIDENCE — P(B)<br/>how common is this evidence overall"]
  evid -->|"="| post["POSTERIOR — P(A∣B)<br/>updated belief after evidence"]

# The Full Formula — Every Symbol With a Soul

P(A∣B)=P(B)P(A)×P(B∣A)​
Symbol Name Plain English
P(A) Prior Your belief before seeing evidence
P(B|A) Likelihood How well the evidence fits your hypothesis
P(B) Marginal How commonly this evidence appears everywhere
P(A|B) Posterior Your updated belief after seeing evidence

# What is P(B) Really?

P(B) is asking: across ALL possible worlds, in how many of them does this evidence appear?

Not “given that you’re sick” — not “given that you’re guilty” — just blindly, across everyone, how often does this evidence show up?

Mathematically it expands like this:

P(B)=from people WITH the hypothesisP(B∣A)P(A)​​+from people WITHOUT the hypothesisP(B∣¬A)P(¬A)​​

It’s the total rate at which the evidence B appears in the entire population — from every source combined.

# The Intuitive Law of P(B)

The more common the evidence, the less it tells you.

If everyone has muddy boots → muddy boots tells you nothing. If almost nobody has muddy boots → muddy boots is a powerful clue.

P(B) is in the denominator precisely because it penalizes common evidence and rewards rare evidence. It’s the universe asking: “Is this clue actually unusual — or does it show up everywhere?”

# The Medical Test

A disease affects 1% of the population. A test is 99% accurate. You test positive. What’s the probability you’re actually sick?

Most people say: “99%! The test is 99% accurate!”

The real answer is 50%. Here’s exactly why —.

# Step 1: Picture 10,000 people

flowchart TD
  pop["10,000 people"] --> dis["100 HAVE the disease (1%)"]
  pop --> well["9,900 DON'T have it (99%)"]
  dis --> tp["99 test POSITIVE ✓<br/>true positives"]
  dis --> fn["1 tests negative<br/>false negative"]
  well --> fp["99 test POSITIVE ✗<br/>false alarms!"]
  well --> tn["9,801 test negative<br/>true negatives"]

# Step 2: What is P(B) here?

P(B) = probability of testing positive at all, regardless of whether you’re sick.

P(B) = P(positive | sick) × P(sick)  +  P(positive | healthy) × P(healthy)
     =       0.99          ×  0.01   +        0.01             ×   0.99
     =       0.0099                  +        0.0099
     =       0.0198

About 2% of all people test positive. That’s P(B). And crucially — it’s being fed from two sources: real sick people AND false alarms from healthy people.

# Step 3: Now apply Bayes

P(sick∣+)=P(B)P(+∣sick)×P(sick)​=0.01980.99×0.01​=0.01980.0099​=50%

# Why did P(B) crush our confidence?

Because P(B) = 0.0198 revealed something brutal: testing positive is not rare. Nearly 2% of the entire healthy population also tests positive (false alarms). The evidence “positive test” is too common to be fully trusted.

Now imagine a more specific test — one with a false positive rate of 0.01% instead of 1%:

P(B) = 0.99 × 0.01  +  0.0001 × 0.99
     = 0.0099        +  0.000099
     = 0.009999  ≈  1%

P(sick | positive) = 0.0099 / 0.009999 ≈ 99%

Same disease. Same prior. But now P(B) is tiny — the evidence “positive test” is rare — so a positive result is enormously informative and your confidence shoots to 99%.

P(B) is the mechanism by which rarity of evidence controls your confidence.

# The Complete Mental Model

flowchart TD
  s(["START"]) --> prior["Your prior belief<br/>how likely is A?"]
  prior --> ev["New evidence B arrives"]
  ev --> fit["How well does B fit A?<br/>P(B∣A) — pushes UP"]
  ev --> common["How common is B in the world?<br/>P(B) — pushes DOWN"]
  fit --> battle["These two forces battle each other"]
  common --> battle
  battle --> post["Your posterior belief<br/>P(A∣B) — the winner"]

# The One-Line Memory Hook

# 🔄 “Start with a guess. See evidence. Ask how rare that evidence is. Make a better guess.”

The prior is your starting guess. The likelihood says how well the evidence fits. P(B) asks: “But how rare is this evidence really?” The posterior is your final, wiser belief.

# Why It Matters

Bayes is used in spam filters, medical diagnosis, self-driving cars, and your own brain. Neuroscientists believe the brain is a Bayesian machine — constantly predicting, observing evidence, and updating. The formula is P(A|B) = P(A)·P(B|A) / P(B). Now every symbol has a soul:

  • P(A) — your belief before
  • P(B|A) — how well evidence fits
  • P(B) — how common that evidence is everywhere, the tax that keeps you honest
  • P(A|B) — your belief after

# 3. Expected Values(Discrete)

# Definition

E[X]=x∑​x⋅P(X=x)

# Key Properties

  • Linearity: E[aX+b]=aE[X]+b
  • Linearity of sum: E[X+Y]=E[X]+E[Y] (always, even if dependent)
  • Product (independent only): E[XY]=E[X]⋅E[Y]
  • Law of Total Expectation: E[X]=E[E[X∣Y]]

# Common Distributions

Distribution E[X]
Bernoulli(p) p
Binomial(n, p) np
Geometric(p) 1/p
Poisson(λ) λ

# Tricky Interview Facts

  • E[f(X)]=f(E[X]) in general — Jensen’s Inequality: if f is convex, E[f(X)]≥f(E[X])
  • Linearity of expectation holds regardless of dependence — commonly tested in combinatorics/probability puzzles (e.g., expected number of fixed points in a random permutation)
  • Expected value can be undefined (e.g., Cauchy distribution) or infinite — relevant when justifying why certain loss functions or estimators behave poorly

# 4. Expected Value (Continuous)

# Definition

E[X]=∫−∞∞​x⋅f(x),dx

where f(x) is the probability density function (PDF).

# Key Properties

Same as discrete — all hold:

  • Linearity: E[aX+b]=aE[X]+b
  • Sum: E[X+Y]=E[X]+E[Y]
  • Product (independent): E[XY]=E[X]⋅E[Y]
  • LOTUS: E[g(X)]=∫−∞∞​g(x)f(x),dx

# Common Distributions

Distribution E[X]
Uniform(a, b) (a+b)/2
Exponential(λ) 1/λ
Normal(μ, σ²) μ
Gamma(α, β) α/β

# Tricky Interview Facts

  • LOTUS (Law of the Unconscious Statistician) — compute E[g(X)] directly from f(x) without finding the distribution of g(X) first; commonly needed for deriving variance E[X2]−(E[X])2
  • For non-negative X: E[X]=∫0∞​P(X>t),dt — useful shortcut, often tested
  • E[X] requires absolute integrability: ∫∣x∣f(x)dx<∞, else undefined — e.g., Cauchy has no mean despite being symmetric around 0

# 5. Central Limit Theorem

The mean of a large sample from any distribution approaches a normal distribution as n → ∞.

Xˉ∼N(μ,nσ2​)
  • Rule of thumb: n ≥ 30 is usually sufficient
  • Variance of the sample mean shrinks by factor n (std dev by √n)

Tricky: CLT applies to the sample mean, not individual samples — the underlying data stays non-normal.

# 6. Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

Core Idea: Find parameters θ that maximize the probability of observing the data.

θ^MLE​=argθmax​i=1∏n​p(xi​∣θ)

In practice, maximize log-likelihood (sum, not product):

θ^MLE​=argθmax​i=1∑n​logp(xi​∣θ)

# Common MLE Results

Distribution MLE Estimate
Gaussian μ sample mean x̄
Gaussian σ² biased variance (1/n)
Bernoulli p sample proportion
Exponential λ 1 / x̄

MLE for Gaussian variance is biased (divides by n, not n−1)

# MLE vs MAP

MLE MAP
Prior Uniform (ignored) Incorporates prior p(θ)
Formula max p(D|θ) max p(D|θ)·p(θ)
Overfitting More prone Regularizes
  • MAP with Gaussian prior → L2 regularization
  • MAP with Laplace prior → L1 regularization

# Connection to Loss Functions

  • MLE under Gaussian noise → minimizing MSE
  • MLE under Bernoulli/cross-entropy → minimizing log loss
  • MLE under Laplace noise → minimizing MAE

# ⚠️ Tricky Interview Facts

  1. MLE Gaussian variance is biased — divides by n not n−1; unbiased estimator uses n−1 (Bessel’s correction)
  2. MLE = ERM — minimizing empirical risk with the right loss is equivalent to MLE under the corresponding noise assumption
  3. MLE can overfit badly with small data — MAP/Bayesian inference preferred; MLE is a special case of MAP with a flat prior

# Likelihood vs Probability

Same math, different question.

Probability Likelihood
Fixed Parameters θ Data X
Varies Data X Parameters θ
Question P(data | θ) L(θ | data)
Sums to 1? ✅ over data ❌ not guaranteed
P(X∣θ)vsL(θ∣X)=P(X∣θ)

Same formula, different interpretation — likelihood is a function of θ, not a probability distribution over θ.

# ⚠️ Tricky Interview Facts

  1. Likelihood does not sum/integrate to 1 over θ — it is NOT a probability distribution over parameters
  2. L(θ|X) = P(X|θ) — numerically identical, conceptually opposite; confusing these is a common interview trap
  3. Likelihood ratios are more meaningful than raw likelihood values — used in hypothesis testing (Neyman-Pearson lemma)
